1. Prepare the Mutton Keema
If using whole mutton, mince it finely in a grinder or ask your butcher for keema. The mince should be fine but not a paste — a slightly coarse texture gives the balls a better bite. Remove any excess fat or sinew before mincing for a cleaner flavour.
2. Grind the Spice Mix
In a dry grinder, coarsely grind the fennel seeds and roasted chana dal into a rough powder. This mixture acts as the key binding and flavouring agent for Kola Urundai, giving it the distinct South Indian taste. Do not grind too fine — a slightly gritty texture helps bind the balls.
3. Mix the Kola
In a large bowl, combine the mutton keema with finely chopped onion, garlic, red chilli powder, black pepper, cumin powder, garam masala, salt and the ground fennel-chana dal mix. Add a generous handful of fresh coriander. Mix everything thoroughly with your hands until well combined. The mixture should be firm enough to roll — if too wet, add a little more ground chana dal.
4. Shape & Rest
Wet your palms lightly with water. Take a small portion of the mixture (about the size of a large marble) and roll firmly between your palms into a smooth, compact ball. Repeat with the remaining mixture. Place the balls on a plate and refrigerate for 20–30 minutes — this firms them up and prevents them from breaking during frying.
5. Deep Fry & Serve
Heat oil in a deep pan to 170–180°C. Gently slide in the mutton balls in small batches — do not overcrowd. Fry for 4–5 minutes, turning occasionally, until deep golden brown and crispy all over. The inside should be fully cooked through. Drain on paper towels. Serve hot with a squeeze of lemon and fresh coriander. Kola Urundai is best eaten immediately while crispy outside and juicy inside.
